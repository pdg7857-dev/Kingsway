# Backlink Strategy

Backlinks remain a primary driver of off-site authority. For a one-person government-procurement-intelligence brand, the realistic path is not mass link-buying or generic outreach; it is earning links from the specific ecosystem this business already touches: trade associations, trade publications, complementary service firms, and digital PR built on original procurement data. Quality and relevance beat volume. This document lays out the achievable, durable tactics.

## Guiding principles

- **Relevance over volume.** A link from a construction trade association or a procurement publication is worth more than dozens of generic directory links.
- **Earn, do not manufacture.** The strongest links come from content and relationships people want to reference, not from schemes that risk penalties.
- **Lead with the assets the site already has:** the deep platform pages, the lead-magnet reports, the calculator, and original award-data analysis.

## 1. Industry associations

Contractors belong to associations, and associations link to useful member resources.

- **Targets:** construction associations, building trades councils, janitorial and building-service contractor associations, facilities-management bodies, landscaping and snow-management associations, security-industry groups, and consulting-engineer associations, in both Canada and the US.
- **The ask:** a member-resource listing or a link from a "helping members find work" page, pointing to a relevant industry page or a free tool (the cost calculator or scorecard).
- **The hook:** the free lead magnets and the calculator are genuinely useful to members, which makes the link easy to justify.

## 2. Trade publications

Trade media constantly need expert contributors and useful tools to cite.

- **Targets:** construction, facilities, janitorial, HVAC, and procurement trade publications and newsletters.
- **Tactics:** contributed expert columns (with a byline link to `/about` or a relevant guide), being quoted as a procurement expert, and having tools or reports cited in articles about winning public work.
- **The hook:** Phil as a named, quotable expert (see the authority-building doc) plus original data they can reference.

## 3. Partnerships with complementary firms

The most strategic links come from firms that serve the same contractors without competing.

- **Bid writers and proposal-development firms** are the ideal partners: they write the bid, this service finds and qualifies the opportunity. The hand-off is natural in both directions.
- **Other complementary partners:** government-registration consultants, certification and set-aside advisors (small-business, Indigenous-business, veteran-owned programs), estimating services, and bonding or surety brokers.
- **The ask:** reciprocal resource links, co-authored content, and referral relationships that include a link. Frame it as "complementary, not competing": you find and qualify the work, they help win it.

## 4. Guest posts

- Pitch genuinely useful, original articles to trade blogs, association newsletters, and partner firms' blogs.
- Topics that travel: how to stop missing government bids, reading a solicitation before you commit, what award data reveals about a trade or region.
- Each guest post earns a contextual link to the most relevant money page or lead magnet and reinforces Phil as the category expert.

## 5. Digital PR around procurement statistics

This is the highest-ceiling tactic and the one the content moat is built to support.

- **The asset:** original analysis of public award data (USASpending for US federal, Canadian disclosure data, and patterns across the platforms and jurisdictions the site already covers). Turn it into a periodic report or data story: who is winning, how much, where demand is growing by trade and region.
- **The play:** package the findings as a referenceable report (the flagship Government Opportunity Intelligence Report is the vehicle), pitch the data story to trade media and procurement reporters, and let journalists and bloggers cite the statistics with a link.
- **Why it works:** original data is the most linkable content type, and it doubles as authority content and lead-magnet fuel. Critically, never fabricate figures; the credibility of the data is the entire value of the tactic.

## 6. Local and industry directories

Lower-effort baseline links that establish the brand entity.

- Reputable business and service directories, procurement-services and consultant directories, and association member directories.
- Keep NAP (name, address or contact, phone or email) consistent with the brand for entity coherence.
- Treat these as a foundation, not the strategy; avoid low-quality link farms entirely.

## Priority and sequencing

| Tier | Tactic | Effort | Link value | When |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Complementary-firm partnerships (bid writers, proposal firms) | Medium | High, highly relevant | Start immediately |
| 1 | Digital PR on award data | High | Highest, authority-building | Once a data asset exists |
| 2 | Trade publication contributions and quotes | Medium | High | Ongoing |
| 2 | Industry association resource links | Low to medium | High relevance | Ongoing |
| 3 | Guest posts | Medium | Medium to high | Ongoing |
| 3 | Reputable directories | Low | Foundation | Early, then leave it |

## What to avoid

- Bought links, link networks, and automated outreach blasts: high risk, low durability.
- Irrelevant directories and comment-spam links: no value, possible harm.
- Over-optimized exact-match anchors from off-site sources: vary anchors naturally, just as the internal-linking strategy prescribes.

The anchor of the whole strategy is that this brand has something genuinely worth linking to: deep platform expertise, free useful tools, complementary partners with aligned incentives, and original data. Build the links on that, and they hold.
